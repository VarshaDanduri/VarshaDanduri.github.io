Metal Shader

Blender · Shader Nodes · Procedural Materials

A procedural metal material built in Blender's shader node editor. The goal was a weathered metallic surface covered in water droplets — built up layer by layer from noise textures, through normal mapping, to a final metallic BSDF.

I started with a Musgrave texture set to multifractal mode, mapped onto the sphere's surface coordinates. This gave a base layer of organic variation — streaky, irregular bright spots against a dark field that could drive surface detail later.

On top of that I layered a Voronoi texture, distorted with a Mapping node and Vector Math to break up the regular cell pattern. The result is soft, blob-like regions across the surface — the shapes that would become individual water droplets.

Feeding the combined noise into a Normal Map node gave the first bump pass. The droplets read as small raised spots on the surface, but the scale was too fine and the shapes too uniform — the green and blue normal visualization shows the tangent-space directions before any metallic shading is applied.

Adjusting the Musgrave scale and dimension settings pushed the bumps larger and more teardrop-shaped, as if water is pooling and running down the surface. At this stage the normal detail alone carries most of the droplet read — each bead catches light on its upper edge and falls into shadow below.

The finished shader plugs the normal map into a metallic BSDF with a dark base color and high roughness variation. Specular highlights on each droplet sit against the brushed metal underneath, giving the sense of condensation on a cold steel surface.
