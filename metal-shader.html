Editable Blender Shader Extension

Blender · MaterialX · OpenUSD · Procedural Materials

Problem

A weathered metallic surface with water droplets is difficult to paint by hand, and one-off shader node graphs do not travel well into a pipeline. Artists need editable parameters, and downstream tools need a material that can export to interchange formats like MaterialX and OpenUSD.

Solution

I built a custom editable shader extension in Blender: a reusable procedural material assembled layer by layer in the node editor, with exposed inputs for scale, roughness, and droplet density. The graph uses Musgrave multifractal for base variation, distorted Voronoi for droplet regions, normal mapping for surface relief, and a metallic BSDF for the final specular response.

The node group is structured so each layer maps cleanly to MaterialX nodes and to UsdPreviewSurface fields for OpenUSD export. Noise-driven normal detail becomes interchangeable height/normal data, and the metallic response aligns with standard roughness/metallic inputs rather than Blender-only node wiring.

I started with a Musgrave texture set to multifractal mode, mapped onto the sphere's surface coordinates. This gave a base layer of organic variation with streaky irregular bright spots against a dark field.

On top of that I layered a Voronoi texture, distorted with a Mapping node and Vector Math to break up the regular cell pattern into soft, blob-like droplet regions.

Feeding the combined noise into a Normal Map node gave the first bump pass. The droplets read as small raised spots, but the scale was too fine and the shapes too uniform.

Adjusting the Musgrave scale and dimension settings pushed the bumps larger and more teardrop-shaped, as if water is pooling and running down the surface.

MaterialX and OpenUSD export

The extension is built for pipeline handoff: procedural layers are grouped and named so they can be translated to MaterialX (mx_noise, mx_bump, standard surface outputs) or baked and assigned through UsdPreviewSurface on OpenUSD export. Exposed inputs on the node group become shader parameters in the exported material definition rather than buried inside the graph.

Outcome

The finished material gives condensation on cold steel entirely from procedural nodes, while staying editable in Blender and structured for export to MaterialX or OpenUSD without rebuilding the look from scratch.
